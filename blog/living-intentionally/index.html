living intentionally

Earlier this year, I got pushed onto a hospital bed. It was a painful infection that demanded a painful surgery (something that deserves a blogpost of its own, someday!). After getting discharged, I was forced to spend a lot of time in my bed. That time was largely spent thinking. Thinking about all the things that got me to this point and where I would like to go next.

After a lot of “soul searching”, some continued physical pain, and a little bit of courage, I decided to slow down in life and take a sabbatical. It’s been roughly a month since then.

The goal of this sabbatical isn’t particularly ambitious. I don’t expect myself to solve the Riemann Hypothesis or become a fitness influencer. What I really want to do is actively push myself towards living a well-led life. In other words, I would like to explore the ideas of living intentionally.

values

If I were 80 years old today, here are the things I would care about:

  1. Did I make meaningful contributions in the database/infrastructure space?
  2. Did I take care of my physical and mental health, in a non-negotiable sense?
  3. Was I a good human being for the people I love?
  4. Did I at least try to solve the Riemann Hypothesis?
  5. Did I achieve financial freedom while enjoying the journey?

That’s it. It’s a pretty small list. However, it has taken roughly 6 years of being an adult to arrive at this. So much of life is spent doing meaningless things.

a lot of life is inherited

For a good chunk of my life, many decisions have happened by default. I studied for JEE (the engineering entrance exam in India, a very competitive one!) because that’s what everyone else was doing. All my friendships in school and college have had the basis that we all were forced to live under the same institution, and we happened to vibe while talking.

I think the first conscicous decision I took in my adult life was to not care deeply about what my college thinks, actively pursue software development outside the standard curriculum, and find a way into Bangalore’s tech ecosystem. And it worked. I rejected a campus placement offer in Pune. I said no to a Gurgaon-based company. And I consciously moved to Bangalore, spending ~3 years building software across fintech, AI, and datacenters. While I was learning the ropes on how real systems work and playing my part in it, I realised over time that I love going down the stack.

obsession and happiness

It took me a while to figure out that working on low-level systems is the most fulfilling activity for me, technically. This sabbatical is special in the sense that:

  1. Nobody is paying me to contribute to open source or exercise.
  2. Nobody would truly care if I stopped tomorrow.

After a long time, I have felt a strong pull of “intrinsic” motivation. Or as Phil Eaton describes it: a healthy obsession.

For big goals or big changes, you need regular commitment weekly, monthly, yearly. Over the course of years. And only obsession makes that work not actually feel like work. Obsession is the only thing that makes discipline not feel like discipline.

The thing about doing what you really want to do is that you feel better overall. I jokingly call this “the glow of unemployment”. I love thinking about systems and solving problems, but maybe that call on Microsoft Teams does make me a little anxious. The absence of Teams in my life for now is a net positive.

And, I think happiness is really important. I don’t think we deeply prioritize our mental well-being as a society. Maybe the post-AGI world will care about it.

snowballing and becoming

Ask ChatGPT or Claude the following question:

“If you could distill my life down to just one metric optimizing for, what would it be?”

It will most likely arrive at an answer that focuses on the ideas of compounding.

I am no expert in this domain. The mathematical simplicity and powerful capability of compounding have already been well stated in this world. What I have missed so far is having an active focus on that. The hope of living intentionally is to compound.

A life well led is a life spent doing. Doing the things you will eventually be defined by. The pursuit makes the man, since identity only emerges from repeated choices.

closing thoughts

There is, somewhat amusingly, an entire Wikipedia page dedicated to intentional living.

Realistically speaking, a lot of things in my overall environment will continue to suck. And my consistency and mood will be challenged to some level. But there is not much that I can do about my country’s poor electricity infrastructure, bad roads, potholes, and lack of political accountability.

But I can design databases and go to the gym.

Maybe, that’s all I need to do.

Happy hacking.

Vivek.
